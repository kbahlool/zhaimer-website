About Zhaimer

Zhaimer is an original strategic memory card game created by Khalid Bahlool under Zhaimer Labs. It is built around a simple challenge: can you remember your hidden cards, reduce your total and choose the right moment to end the round?

The game combines memory, observation, timing and calculated risk. New players can understand the main idea quickly, while experienced players learn that every draw creates a decision. A useful card can improve your hand, reveal important information or disrupt an opponent. A careless move can add points or expose a weakness.

The idea behind the game

Zhaimer was designed to turn the uncertainty of face-down cards into a social and competitive experience. Players do not have complete information. They must remember what they saw, watch what other players do and decide how much confidence to place in their own memory.

The name reflects the playful tension at the centre of the game: remembering the position and value of your cards is often the difference between a strong round and an unexpected result.

Objective

Your goal is to finish the round with the lowest total card value. Low cards are generally helpful, while high cards should be replaced or discarded whenever possible. Across a full match, points accumulate, and a player who reaches 100 points is eliminated. The aim is therefore not only to win one round, but to manage risk consistently over several rounds.

Starting a round

  1. Each player receives four face-down cards.
  2. At the beginning, each player may look at two of their cards for four seconds.
  3. The cards are hidden again, and players must rely on memory unless a special ability allows another reveal.
  4. Players take turns drawing and using cards according to the options available in the game.

Because most cards remain hidden, it is useful to remember both their values and their positions. Even when you know you have a low card, forgetting where it is can change your next decision.

Card values

  • Number cards normally count as the number printed on the card.
  • Picture cards count as 10 points unless they are being used for their special ability.
  • The 10 of Hearts and the 10 of Diamonds are special zero-point cards.
  • The 10 of Clubs and the 10 of Spades remain 10-point cards and do not form the same special discard pair as the red tens.

The two red tens are especially valuable because they reduce your total without appearing low at first glance. Remembering their location can create a major advantage.

Special cards

  • King — Draw Two: gives the player a draw-two opportunity, creating more choices for improving the hidden hand.
  • Jack — Swap: allows cards to be exchanged. A well-timed swap can remove a suspected high card or place uncertainty into an opponent’s hand.
  • Queen — Reveal: allows one hidden card to be viewed. This can confirm your memory before an important replacement or before declaring the end of the round.

Matching and discarding

Identical card numbers can be discarded when the game allows a matching attempt. This is sometimes called “burning” a pair. Successful matching reduces the number of cards in play and can lower your final total.

A failed matching attempt carries a penalty: the cards are returned and the player draws two cards. This makes guessing dangerous. The strongest matching decisions are based on something you have seen or carefully remembered rather than chance.

Declaring the end of a round

When a player believes they hold the lowest total, they may declare that they are finished. The declaration does not end the round immediately. The other players receive one final turn until play returns to the person who made the declaration. Then all remaining cards are revealed and the totals are calculated.

Declaring early can protect a strong hand, but declaring too soon can give opponents one last opportunity to improve. Waiting too long is also risky because another player may finish first.

Beginner strategy

  • Memorize positions, not only values. Think “top-left is 3” rather than remembering a loose list.
  • Replace cards you know are high before gambling on cards you have forgotten.
  • Use the Queen when the information will affect an immediate decision.
  • Watch opponents’ reactions and swaps; their choices may reveal which positions they value.
  • Do not attempt a match unless you have a reasonable memory of both cards.
  • Before declaring, estimate your total and consider how many opponents still have a final turn.

The browser edition

Zhaimer is available as a browser-based game, so visitors can open it without installing a separate application. The project is being developed gradually, and features, game modes, visuals and statistics may be refined as new versions are released.

Leaderboard information may be stored locally in the player’s browser. Clearing browser data or changing devices can therefore remove local records. No account is required for the current local experience.

Future development

Zhaimer Labs plans to continue improving tutorials, visual clarity, accessibility, computer-player behaviour, statistics and competitive features. The long-term goal is to make Zhaimer easy to start, rewarding to master and enjoyable on both desktop and mobile screens.

Frequently asked questions

Is Zhaimer free to play?

Yes. The current browser version can be played from the website. Visitors may support the project voluntarily through PayPal.

Do I need to create an account?

No account is required for the current browser experience.

Can I play on a phone?

The website is designed to work in modern mobile browsers. For the best experience, use an updated version of Chrome, Edge, Safari or Firefox.
